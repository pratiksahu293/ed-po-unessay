Youth of Color Transforming Power Through Digital Media

I argue that youth media engagement is a source of power—not harm. Through digital platforms, young people challenge stereotypes, organize resistance, and build identity outside traditional institutions.

The Issue

The Issue

Youth of color are often represented in the media through negative or limiting stereotypes.

News stories, movies, and even school policies frequently frame young people as dangerous, failing, or “at risk” instead of creative, thoughtful, or capable leaders.

These portrayals shape how teachers, adults, and society treat youth — and they can also affect how young people see themselves.

My Position

My Position

I argue that youth media engagement is a source of power, not harm.

When young people create content, share experiences, and connect with others online, they are not wasting time — they are building identity, confidence, and political awareness.

Youth are not passive consumers of media. They actively shape narratives and challenge systems that try to silence them.

What I Found

My research shows that youth of color use digital media in powerful ways.

Through videos, hashtags, comments, and storytelling, young people:

  • Challenge stereotypes placed on their communities
  • Share lived experiences that are ignored by mainstream media
  • Educate peers about racism, identity, and injustice
  • Organize protests, fundraisers, and awareness campaigns

Digital platforms function as spaces for learning, organizing, and self-expression — not just entertainment.

Why It Matters

Why It Matters

When youth voices are dismissed, harmful narratives remain unchallenged.

Recognizing young people as creators and leaders helps schools, communities, and adults better support youth development.

If educators and policymakers listen to how youth already use media, they can create learning environments that are more inclusive, empowering, and relevant to young people’s lives.
